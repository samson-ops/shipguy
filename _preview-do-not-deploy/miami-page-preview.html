Quick answerShipping a standard sedan to or from Miami typically costs $450–$1,600 on open transport depending on distance, with pickup usually within 1–5 days of booking and delivery in 1–10 days by route. Enclosed transport runs roughly 40–60% more. During snowbird season (Oct–Dec southbound, Apr–May northbound), rates in the peak direction rise 20–40% — get an exact price for your route in about two minutes.

What Shapes Your Miami Auto Transport Price

Five factors set most of your quote: distance and route popularity, vehicle size and weight, open vs. enclosed transport, season, and date flexibility. The I-95 corridor is Florida's cheapest lane per mile because carriers run it constantly — and if your pickup window can flex by two or three days, we can usually place your vehicle at a lower rate than a guaranteed-date booking.

Southbound peak • Oct–Dec

Seasonal residents ship cars into South Florida ahead of winter. Southbound rates rise 20–40%, while northbound trucks leave half-empty at a discount.

Northbound peak • Apr–May

The migration reverses in spring. Northbound prices spike as vehicles head home, and southbound becomes the bargain direction.

What it means for you: shipping against the seasonal flow often costs well under the averages above. Shipping with it? Book 2–3 weeks ahead — waiting until the week you fly out is the most expensive mistake snowbirds make. See our snowbird shipping guide →

Open vs. Enclosed Auto Transport: Which Is Right for Your Miami Shipment?

It's the first real decision you'll make, and in Miami the answer isn't always obvious — this market ships more high-value vehicles than almost any other city in the country.

Most popular • ~90% of shipments

Open Transport

The two-level carriers you see delivering to dealerships. Your vehicle rides exposed to weather and road dust but fully secured — the same method manufacturers use to move brand-new cars from the factory. A brief South Florida rain shower in transit is a car wash issue, not a damage issue.

  • Best for daily drivers, SUVs, trucks, and most family vehicles
  • Lowest price on every lane, and fastest pickup — far more open trucks run I-95 than enclosed
  • Typical carrier cargo insurance: $100K–$250K per load
Premium protection

Enclosed Transport

A fully covered trailer, usually with a hydraulic liftgate for low-clearance vehicles. Enclosed demand in Miami is unusually high — between the exotics in Brickell, the collector garages in Coral Gables, and auction buys headed to restoration shops, we place enclosed loads on South Florida lanes every week.

  • Best for exotics, classics, restored vehicles, and anything low to the ground
  • Runs roughly 40–60% above open rates
  • Higher cargo insurance limits, typically $250K–$1M

Our honest rule of thumb: if you'd drive it in a rainstorm without thinking twice, ship it open and keep the difference. If the vehicle is worth well into six figures, sits low enough to scrape a driveway apron, or just left a restoration shop, the enclosed premium is cheap insurance. Compare details on our open transport and enclosed transport pages, or ask us — we'll tell you straight if enclosed is overkill for your vehicle.

What Actually Happens at Pickup and Delivery

The three steps above are the short version. Here's the part most companies don't explain until the truck is outside.

The day before pickup

Your assigned driver calls to confirm a window — usually two to four hours, not a precise minute, because they're working a multi-stop route through South Florida traffic. If your street can't fit a 75-foot rig, this is when you'll agree on a nearby meeting spot: a shopping plaza on US-1, a wide side street, wherever is safe and legal to load.

At pickup: the Bill of Lading

Before your car goes on the truck, the driver walks around it and records its condition — every existing scratch, ding, and scuff — with photos, on a document called the Bill of Lading. Walk the car with them. This document is your protection: it's the before picture that any damage claim gets measured against. Take your own photos too, in good light, all four corners plus the roof. Then hand over one key (keep your spare), and the driver loads and secures the vehicle.

During transit

You'll have the driver's direct cell number. Long-haul drivers legally drive up to 11 hours a day, so a Miami-to-New-York run genuinely takes about three days — radio silence for a few hours usually means they're driving, not that something's wrong. Ship Guy stays reachable the whole way if you'd rather check in with us.

At delivery: inspect before you sign

Walk the car again against the pickup Bill of Lading, in daylight if at all possible. If everything matches — and the overwhelming majority of the time it does — you sign, settle any balance due with the driver by the method confirmed at booking, and you're done. If something doesn't match, note it on the Bill of Lading before signing and photograph it. That notation is what activates the carrier's cargo insurance; signing clean first and calling about damage later makes a claim far harder to win.

Preparing Your Car for Transport: The Miami Checklist

Ten minutes of prep prevents ninety percent of shipping headaches. A few of these are Florida-specific.

  • Pull your SunPass or toll transponder. The single most common Florida shipping mistake. If it stays on the windshield, it can get read at every toll gantry the carrier passes on the Turnpike and beyond — and those charges land on your account. Take it out, or at minimum seal it in the foil bag it came with.
  • Wash the car before pickup. Not for vanity — a clean car makes the Bill of Lading inspection accurate. Existing scratches get documented properly, and new damage (rare) can't hide under a layer of dirt.
  • Leave about a quarter tank of gas. Enough to load, unload, and drive off at delivery. A full tank adds weight the carrier doesn't want and you don't need to pay to move.
  • Remove or secure everything loose. Garage remotes, parking placards, dash cams, and anything in door pockets. Fold in your mirrors if they don't do it automatically, and retract the antenna if yours is the old-school kind.
  • Disable the alarm, or show the driver how to kill it. An alarm that triggers on trailer vibration somewhere in Georgia is nobody's favorite phone call.
  • Flag any quirks in writing. Weak battery, tricky ignition, leak, aftermarket kill switch, air suspension that needs to be in transport mode — tell us at booking and remind the driver at pickup so they can load it safely.
  • Photograph everything, including the odometer. Your photos plus the driver's Bill of Lading photos means there's never an argument about condition or mileage.

Shipping something unusual, inoperable, or heavily modified? See our full vehicle preparation guide or call us and we'll walk you through it.

How to Vet Any Auto Transport Company (Including Us)

Miami's shipping volume attracts good operators and bad ones. Whether you book with Ship Guy or anyone else, run these checks — they take five minutes and they're free.

Verify the license, don't take the website's word for it

Every legitimate broker and carrier has an MC number registered with the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration. Look it up yourself in the FMCSA SAFER database: confirm the authority is active, the company name matches who you're actually talking to, and — for brokers — the required $75,000 surety bond is on file. That bond exists specifically to protect you if a broker takes your money and fails to perform. A company that gets cagey when you ask for its MC number has answered your question.

Be suspicious of the lowest quote, not the highest

The classic bait works like this: quote $300 under everyone else, collect the booking, then let the order sit because no real carrier will run the lane at that price. A week later comes the call — "we found a truck, but it's going to be a bit more." You've lost your pickup window and your leverage. If one quote is dramatically below the pack, it isn't a deal; it's a number designed to win your booking, not to ship your car. Real Miami lane rates cluster in a fairly tight band at any given moment, which is exactly why we publish estimated ranges on this page.

Three more red flags worth walking away from

  • A large deposit demanded before a carrier is assigned. The industry-standard structure charges you nothing, or next to nothing, until there's an actual truck with your vehicle's name on it.
  • No written insurance certificate on request. You're entitled to see the assigned carrier's cargo insurance certificate before pickup. "Don't worry, everything's covered" is not a certificate.
  • Reviews that only exist on the company's own website. Check Google, BBB, and Transport Reviews, and read the one-star reviews specifically — how a company behaves when something goes wrong tells you more than a hundred five-star summaries.

Six Ways to Pay Less for Auto Transport in Miami

  • Flex your dates by two or three days. The single biggest lever you control. A flexible pickup window lets us place your car on a truck that's already running your lane instead of paying a premium to pull one in.
  • Ship against the snowbird current. Northbound out of Miami is discounted October through December; southbound is the bargain in April and May. If your move can happen off-cycle, the calendar does the negotiating for you.
  • Book 2–3 weeks ahead of peak windows — but not months ahead. Carriers price loads close to the run date, so booking half a year early doesn't lock in a better rate. Two to three weeks out hits the sweet spot of choice and price during busy season.
  • Meet the truck somewhere easy. If you're in a high-rise or on a tight street, offering to meet at a big lot near a highway exit makes your load more attractive to carriers — and cheaper to serve.
  • Skip guaranteed-date pricing unless you truly need it. A guaranteed pickup date is a real product with a real premium. If "sometime Tuesday to Thursday" works for your life, don't pay for "Tuesday at 10 a.m."
  • Compare all-in numbers, not teaser numbers. When you shop quotes, ask each company one question: "Is this the total I'll pay, including the driver's portion at delivery?" It's remarkable how many low quotes grow when that question gets asked.

Miami Car Shipping FAQ

Common Questions

How much does it cost to ship a car to or from Miami?
For a standard sedan on open transport, typical ranges are $450–$700 to the Southeast, $700–$1,100 to the Northeast or Midwest, and $1,100–$1,600 cross-country. Enclosed transport runs roughly 40–60% more. Your exact price depends on vehicle size, address accessibility, and season — South Florida rates move with snowbird demand. The fastest way to get an accurate number is to request a free quote.
How long does it take to ship a car to Miami?
Transit time depends mainly on distance. A move from Atlanta typically runs a couple of days, the Northeast is usually in the three-to-six day range, and a cross-country trip from the West Coast generally takes about a week or more. Weather, traffic, and route availability can shift these estimates.
Do you offer door-to-door pickup in Miami?
Yes. Whenever possible we arrange door-to-door pickup and delivery at your Miami address. Some areas — narrow streets in Coconut Grove or Little Havana, gated communities, and low-clearance parking garages downtown or in Brickell — can't safely fit a full-size carrier, so the driver will arrange a nearby meeting point such as a large lot.
When is the busiest season for car shipping in South Florida?
Demand peaks around the snowbird season. Vehicles move south into Florida heading into the fall and winter, and back north in the spring. Booking earlier during those windows generally means better pricing and more scheduling flexibility. See our snowbird car shipping page for details.
Can you ship a car that doesn't run?
Yes, but the carrier needs to know in advance. A non-running vehicle requires equipment such as a winch and a driver prepared to load it, so mention it when you request your quote and we'll match you with a carrier set up for inoperable vehicles.
Do you ship anything besides cars in Miami?
Yes. Along with vehicles, Ship Guy arranges transport for motorcycles, boats, freight and pallets, furniture, household goods, heavy equipment, and oversized items to and from the Miami area.
Can I put personal items in the car?
Most carriers allow up to about 100 lbs of items packed in the trunk or below window level. Personal items aren't covered by the carrier's cargo insurance, so never ship valuables, documents, or anything irreplaceable inside the vehicle.
Do I need to be present for pickup or delivery?
No. Any adult you authorize — a family member, friend, or building manager — can release or accept the vehicle and sign the inspection paperwork, which is why many snowbird customers ship their car a week before or after they fly.
Is my vehicle insured during transport?
Yes. The assigned carrier's cargo insurance covers your vehicle in transit, and the driver documents its condition with photos on the Bill of Lading at pickup and delivery. You can request the carrier's insurance certificate before pickup, and your own auto policy typically remains secondary coverage.
